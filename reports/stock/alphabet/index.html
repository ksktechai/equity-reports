01 — Executive summary

A regulated advertising monopoly, re-rated as an AI infrastructure story

Alphabet is the holding company for Google. It makes the overwhelming majority of its money selling advertising against the world's most-used search engine and YouTube, while Google Cloud and a subscriptions business have become large, fast-growing second and third engines. After a 2025 of record results and a Q1 2026 cloud surge, the market has paid up.

Bull thesis · one line

AI is expanding, not eroding, the moat

Gemini-powered Search, a $462B cloud backlog and 60%+ cloud growth suggest Alphabet monetizes the AI shift across search, YouTube and enterprise — while still printing $70B+ of free cash flow.
Bear thesis · one line

~$185B of 2026 capex into a contested moat

An adverse antitrust remedy, AI chat displacing high-margin search clicks, and a doubling of capex that hasn't yet shown up as higher free cash flow could compress both growth and the multiple at once.
What changed most recently

Q1 2026: cloud +63%, capex doubling

Revenue $109.9B (+22%), Google Cloud $20.0B (+63%) at a 32.9% margin, GenAI-product revenue up ~800% YoY — paired with guidance toward ~$185B of 2026 capital spending.
6/10 Analysis Complexity Score: the business is simple to describe but hard to value — earnings are clean and disclosed, yet the antitrust overhang, the AI-vs-search debate, and a capex cycle that distorts free cash flow make the forward picture genuinely uncertain. OPINION
02 — Business model

How Alphabet makes money

Roughly 85% of revenue is Google Services — advertising plus subscriptions, devices and platforms. Google Cloud is ~15% and now profitable. Other Bets (Waymo, Verily, etc.) is a rounding error in revenue and a structural loss. Economics improve with scale: a global ad auction and a built-out data-center fleet carry very high incremental margins.

03 — Financial trajectory

06 — Governance & management

Brilliant capital allocation, shareholder-unfriendly control

The operating track record is excellent; the governance structure is not. Founders Larry Page and Sergey Brin retain voting control through super-voting Class B shares, so public Class A (GOOGL) and non-voting Class C (GOOG) holders effectively cannot change the company's direction. You are buying a phenomenal business and accepting that you do not control it.

Dual-class voting

Class B (Page & Brin) carries 10 votes/share and retains majority control. GOOGL = 1 vote; GOOG = 0 votes. Minority holders cannot force change. A structural governance negative.

Capital-allocation record

Strong: organic R&D and disciplined M&A (YouTube, Android, DeepMind) created enormous value; few destructive megadeals. Buybacks have been steady and accretive.

Management & tenure

CEO Sundar Pichai (Google 2015, Alphabet 2019) has navigated the AI transition credibly. Deep bench; founders remain involved as controlling shareholders and board members.
5/10 Governance Score: excellent operating stewardship and capital allocation, dragged down hard by entrenched dual-class control, limited minority rights and a history of large regulatory run-ins. Great managers, weak shareholder protections. OPINION

10 — Bull vs bear

The two-sided case, fact by fact

🟢 Bull case

  • Cloud is inflecting. +63% in Q1'26 at a 32.9% margin with a ~$462B backlog — a second growth engine that is now also a profit engine.
  • Search isn't breaking. Search & other grew ~17% in 2025 with AI Overviews live — evidence AI is augmenting, not gutting, the core auction.
  • Cash generation is elite. $70B+ FCF even at peak capex, net cash, and a tiny payout ratio leave huge optionality.
  • Relative value. ~26× forward is a discount to the ~30× mega-cap median despite comparable growth and superior scale.
  • Distribution moat. Chrome, Android, Search, YouTube and Maps each have 1B+ users — unmatched surfaces to deploy AI products.
  • Proprietary AI stack. Gemini models plus in-house TPUs give cost and capability advantages few rivals can match end to end.

🔴 Bear case

  • Antitrust is existential. US courts have found monopolies in search and ad-tech; remedies could unwind default-search deals or the ad stack itself.
  • Search cannibalization risk. Even if Alphabet wins AI, chat answers may generate fewer high-margin clicks per query than the legacy ten-blue-links model.
  • FCF has gone flat. ~2% FCF CAGR over four years as capex exploded — the income statement grows, the cash statement doesn't.
  • $185B capex is unproven. Roughly doubling spend bets enormous capital on AI demand that could soften, leaving stranded assets and depreciation.
  • The discount has shrunk. After +120% in a year, the stock no longer offers the deep-value cushion that defined its prior thesis.
  • You don't get a vote. Dual-class control means minority holders have no lever if capital allocation or strategy goes wrong.

12 — Verdict
Author's opinion · not financial advice
Buy
A rare combination of dominant economics and a credible AI position — but bought after a big run, so size for valuation and regulatory risk, not for a bargain.

Alphabet is one of the highest-quality businesses in the world: a near-monopoly search-advertising engine, an inflecting and now-profitable cloud, elite margins and a net-cash balance sheet. The 2025–26 results show AI is so far expanding the moat rather than eroding it, and at ~26× forward earnings the stock still sits at a discount to mega-cap peers. The reasons to hesitate are real and specific — an unresolved antitrust overhang that could strike the core, a ~$185B capex bet that hasn't yet converted into free-cash-flow growth, and a valuation that, post-120% rally, no longer offers a wide margin of safety. On balance the quality and relative value tip it to a Buy, but a measured one.

Biggest reason bulls may be right

If AI Overviews and Gemini keep monetizing search while Cloud compounds 30%+ at rising margins, Alphabet is the best-positioned AI compounder in mega-cap — and is the cheapest of them on forward earnings.

Biggest reason bears may be right

A forced antitrust remedy or AI-driven decline in high-margin search clicks could hit the core profit engine and the multiple at the same time — exactly when $185B of capex is depressing free cash flow.
