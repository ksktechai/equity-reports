01

The one-paragraph version

D-Wave (NYSE: QBTS) builds and sells access to quantum computers. For two decades it has been the leader in quantum annealing — a specialised approach suited to optimisation problems — sold both as on-prem systems and as cloud "Quantum-Computing-as-a-Service" (QCaaS) via its Leap platform. In 2025 revenue jumped 179% to $24.6M, lifted by an eight-figure first system sale. But that lumpiness cut the other way in Q1'26: revenue fell 81% to just $2.9M, while bookings — future contracted demand — surged 1,994% to $33.4M (a $20M Florida Atlantic University system plus a $10M Fortune 100 QCaaS deal). In January 2026 D-Wave agreed to acquire Quantum Circuits Inc. for $550M to accelerate an error-corrected gate-model roadmap, and at its first-ever Investor Day (1 Jun 2026) it laid out milestones reaching a 100-logical-qubit fault-tolerant system by 2032. The company is richly funded (~$588M cash) but deeply unprofitable (~$100M annual free-cash burn) and valued at roughly 670× trailing sales.

How D-Wave makes money

Two revenue streams, one core technology. QCaaS — recurring cloud access to D-Wave's annealing systems via the Leap platform — is the steadier, higher-margin base. System sales & professional services — selling an entire Advantage2 machine (e.g. to Florida Atlantic University, Davidson Technologies/Forschungszentrum Jülich) — are large but lumpy, landing in one quarter and distorting growth optics. DOES THE MODEL IMPROVE WITH SCALE?

The unit economics could be excellent — cloud access to a quantum machine is near-pure software margin once the hardware exists, and FY2025 gross margin of ~83% hints at that. The problem is the numerator: total revenue is ~$12–25M, far below the ~$120M+ of annual operating spend. There is no scale yet — D-Wave is spending multiples of revenue to fund R&D and a go-to-market build, so operating losses widen even as gross margin looks pristine. Whether quantum optimisation finds a "killer app" with broad, repeatable demand is still unproven. Confidence: Med

THE NUMBER THAT MATTERS MOST — REVENUE vs MARKET CAP

D-Wave carries an ~$8–9B market cap on ~$12M of trailing revenue — roughly 670× sales — for a business whose revenue just fell 81% in the latest quarter and which burns ~$100M of cash a year. Even on the record $33.4M quarterly bookings annualised (~$134M), the multiple is ~60×+. The bull's counter is that quantum is a winner-take-most frontier and bookings, not GAAP revenue, signal the inflection. The bear's point is simpler: the price embeds a future that is years away and far from certain.

04

Balance sheet & capital returns

This is the strongest part of the story. D-Wave used 2025's surging share price to raise huge sums through at-the-market (ATM) equity offerings, building cash and investments to $588.4M at 31 Mar 2026 against just ~$47M of debt — net cash of ~$542M ($1.46/share). That war chest buys years of runway at the current ~$100M burn and funds the $250M cash portion of the Quantum Circuits deal. The cost: relentless dilution. Shares outstanding rose from ~125M (2021) to ~321M (2025) to ~370M now — roughly 3× in four years. There is no dividend and no buyback; capital is raised, not returned.

THE DILUTION TRADE-OFF

D-Wave's funding strategy is rational but punishing for per-share value: it sells stock when the price is high to bank cash, then uses that cash to survive and to buy capability (Quantum Circuits). The result is genuine financial strength at the company level and persistent erosion at the per-share level — a ~3× rise in share count means the business must grow ~3× just to keep per-share value flat. Financial Strength Score: 6 / 10 — abundant cash and almost no debt, offset by heavy burn and chronic dilution. Confidence: High on the balance sheet; Med on burn trajectory

Governance & management

A credible technologist-CEO who has steered the company through a near-death balance sheet to a fortress one — but with thin insider ownership, steady insider selling, and a capital-allocation history defined by heavy dilution.

Strengths

What's good

President & CEO Dr. Alan Baratz (in the seat since 2020, ex-IBM/JavaSoft) has executed a textbook survival-to-strength funding playbook: tapping equity markets aggressively at high prices to amass ~$588M of cash and acquire gate-model capability when the company could easily have run out of money. The Investor Day showed a coherent dual-platform strategy and a public technology roadmap. The company has a single class of common stock — no dual-class vote — so public holders' shares carry full voting rights.

Watch items

What to watch

Insider ownership is low — Baratz holds ~0.53% — and insiders have been net sellers (Baratz sold ~$18M in June 2026, citing tax/financial planning). The defining capital-allocation choice has been issuing stock: a ~3× rise in share count in four years. The $550M Quantum Circuits deal is a large bet on unproven gate-model tech whose payoff is years out and hard to verify. Governance Score: 5 / 10 — clean share structure and capable leadership, weighed against thin alignment and dilution. Confidence: Med

A "remarkable technology, indefensible price" call — for the valuation, not the science. D-Wave is a genuine quantum-computing pioneer with the cohort's clearest commercial footing, a record bookings quarter, a fortress balance sheet, and a coherent new dual-platform strategy. Those strengths are why this isn't a Strong Sell. But the stock changes hands at roughly 670× trailing sales for a business whose revenue just fell 81%, which burns ~$100M a year, triples its share count over four years, and rests on a commercial quantum-advantage case that remains unproven and a fault-tolerance roadmap stretching to 2032. This is explicitly not personalised advice and we are not licensed advisors; a believer in the quantum theme and the bookings inflection could rate this "Buy," while a valuation- or dilution-focused investor could rate it "Strong Sell."

Biggest reason bulls may be right

If quantum computing crosses from science project to commercial tool this decade, D-Wave — already shipping systems, with record bookings, a war chest to outlast rivals, and now both annealing and gate-model platforms — is among the best-positioned independents, and a tiny revenue base could compound fast enough to make today's multiple look prescient.

Biggest reason bears may be right

The market is paying ~$8–9B for ~$12M of shrinking revenue and ~$100M of annual losses, funded by tripling the share count, on a bet that pays off — if at all — years from now. If the theme cools or the demand inflection stalls, a profitless stock priced for perfection has a very long way to fall.
