01

The one-paragraph version

Cerebras builds the Wafer Scale Engine (WSE-3) — a single AI chip the size of an entire silicon wafer, with ~4 trillion transistors and 900,000 cores — and the CS-3 systems and inference/training cloud built around it, pitched as far faster than clusters of Nvidia GPUs for AI inference. Revenue exploded from $79M (2023) to $290M (2024) to $510M (2025), and the company reported $238M of GAAP net income for 2025 — but that figure is created by a one-time $363M gain from restructuring its G42 contract; strip it out and Cerebras ran a ~$76M non-GAAP operating loss that widened year-on-year. The stock IPO'd at $185 on 13 May 2026, nearly doubled on debut, and now sits ~$235 for a ~$51B market cap — about 100× trailing sales.

What Cerebras actually sells

One radical idea: instead of cutting a wafer into hundreds of small GPUs, Cerebras keeps the whole wafer as a single giant chip. It monetises that three ways — selling CS-3 hardware, selling AI supercomputers/data-center deployments, and renting the compute as an inference/training cloud. DO ECONOMICS IMPROVE WITH SCALE?

Not yet visibly. Gross margin fell from ~42% (2024) to ~39% (2025) as data-center build-out costs rose, and the company still posts non-GAAP operating losses. Management's stated path to better margins is (a) mix-shift toward higher-margin cloud/inference revenue and (b) the WSE-4 transition to TSMC's N3 process. Both are forward bets, not demonstrated operating leverage. Whether wafer-scale economics beat the GPU cost curve at scale is still the central unproven question. Confidence: Med

Governance & management

A strong operator at the helm, wrapped in a governance structure that gives public shareholders very little control and embeds related-party complexity at the heart of the revenue base.

Strengths

What's good

Co-founder/CEO Andrew Feldman is a proven semiconductor entrepreneur — he previously founded SeaMicro and sold it to AMD — and has led Cerebras since 2015 through a genuinely novel architecture and a landmark OpenAI win. Founder alignment is high; insiders kept large stakes through the IPO (the listing reportedly minted two billionaires).

Watch items

What to watch

Dual-class structure: Class B shares carry 20 votes each vs 1 for public Class A, concentrating voting control with founders/insiders (reports put aggregate insider voting power near ~99% post-IPO; Feldman's own economic stake is far smaller — figures conflict, treat with caution). Add G42-related related-party history, the CFIUS review the IPO had to clear, and UAE customer concentration, and outside holders have little leverage. Governance Score: 3 / 10. Confidence: Med

A "fascinating but un-ownable at this price" call, not a quality dismissal. Cerebras has a genuinely novel chip, explosive growth and a marquee OpenAI anchor — the reasons the score isn't lower. But at ~100× sales with operating losses, ~86% revenue concentration in two UAE-linked accounts, a 20-vote founder structure and an early lock-up days from the first-ever public earnings, the risk/reward is too binary to chase. Biggest reason bulls may be right

If wafer-scale inference is real and the OpenAI $20B+ deal converts, Cerebras becomes the scarce, investable #2 to Nvidia in the fastest-growing market in technology — and a $510M revenue base could compound into the billions, making today's multiple look reasonable in hindsight.

Biggest reason bears may be right

The market is paying ~100× sales for a loss-making company whose revenue rests on two related Gulf-state customers and one mega-deal, against the most formidable software-ecosystem moat in chips. If any leg wobbles — concentration, the OpenAI ramp, or margins — there is enormous air beneath the stock.
