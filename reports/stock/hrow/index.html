01

Executive summary

Harrow discovers, licenses, buys and commercialises ophthalmic pharmaceuticals in the US. It makes money two ways: (1) branded prescription drugs sold through pharmacies, buy-and-bill and specialty channels, and (2) ImprimisRx, an ophthalmology-focused compounding pharmacy. The 2023–24 licensing of legacy Novartis/Santen eye brands plus the ramp of VEVYE for dry-eye disease turned Harrow from a niche compounder into a ~$270M-revenue specialty pharma.

Bull thesis · one line

A high-growth, high-gross-margin eyecare portfolio led by VEVYE (tracking to >$100M in 2026) with operating leverage that should flip the P&L to real profitability as the branded mix scales.

Bear thesis · one line

A serially loss-making, ~$292M-levered roll-up whose gross-to-net revenue "adjustments" and guidance history make the path to durable free cash flow — and to servicing 8.625% notes — far from proven.

What changed most recently — Q1 2026 (reported May 2026)

Revenue of $44.2M fell 7.6% YoY and missed the ~$52.6M consensus by ~16%, dragged by an ~$8M non-recurring VEVYE gross-to-net adjustment. Gross margin slipped to 61% (from 68%), net loss widened to −$27.6M and Adjusted EBITDA swung to −$12.7M. Yet management reaffirmed full-year guidance of $350–365M revenue and $80–100M Adjusted EBITDA, and guided Q2 to $71–81M — implying a very back-half-loaded year. Unit demand stayed strong (IHEEZO +18%, TRIESENCE +136% YoY).

Business model

Two engines. Branded Rx is the growth story and the margin story; ImprimisRx compounding is the older, steadier base that seeded Harrow's ophthalmology franchise and prescriber relationships. Harrow does not run its own large-scale manufacturing for most brands — it licenses/acquires approved products and commercialises them, which keeps the model asset-light but reliant on deal-making.
